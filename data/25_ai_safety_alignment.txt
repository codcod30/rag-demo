AI Safety and Alignment

AI alignment is the challenge of ensuring that AI systems behave in accordance with human values and intentions, especially as systems become more capable.

Why Alignment Is Hard:
Specifying what we want from an AI system is difficult because human values are complex, context-dependent, and sometimes self-contradictory. When we optimize a system for a proxy objective, it may find ways to maximize that proxy that diverge from our true intent, a phenomenon called specification gaming or reward hacking. Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

Key Alignment Approaches:

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback):
Humans rank model outputs; a reward model is trained to predict these preferences; the LLM is fine-tuned via PPO to maximize the reward model's score. Used to create ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini.

Constitutional AI (Anthropic):
The model is trained to critique its own outputs against a set of principles (a "constitution") and revise them. Reduces reliance on human labeling for each behavior.

Interpretability and Mechanistic Interpretability:
Understand what computations are happening inside neural networks. Tools like sparse autoencoders attempt to identify monosemantic features. Understanding model internals is essential for verifying alignment and detecting deceptive behavior.

Robustness and Red-Teaming:
Systematically try to elicit harmful behaviors from AI systems to identify and fix vulnerabilities before deployment.

Scalable Oversight:
As AI systems become more capable than humans in some domains, how can humans verify their outputs? Approaches include debate (two AI agents argue for opposite conclusions; humans judge) and recursive reward modeling.
